We model this experiment with a Multinomial distribution where X_j is the jth restaurant n is the number of observations \(\theta_{i,j}\) is the ith star review of the jth observation: j = 1,2, 30 i = 1,2,3,4,5

\[X_{1} \sim Multinomial(n_{1};\theta_{1,1}, \theta_{1,2}, \theta_{1,3}, \theta_{1,4}, \theta_{1,5})\] \[X_{2} \sim Multinomial(n_{2};\theta_{2,1}, \theta_{2,2}, \theta_{2,3}, \theta_{2,4}, \theta_{2,5})\] all the way up to the 30th restaurant: \[X_{30} \sim Multinomial(n_{30};\theta_{30,1}, \theta_{30,2}, \theta_{30,3}, \theta_{30,4}, \theta_{30,5})\]

\[H_{0} : \theta_{1,i} = \theta_{2,i} = \theta_{3,i} = \theta_{4,i} = \theta_{5,i} \]

\[H_{A} : \theta_{1,i} != \theta_{2,i}! = \theta_{3,i} != \theta_{4,i} != \theta_{5,i} \]

Our null hypothesis states that each restaurant is equally likely to be given a certain rating (i.e.5 stars)

There is evidence that 4 star reviews are associated with 5 star ones, having a p-value of 0.00282. Three star reviews are somewhat significant with a p-value of 0.03599. The rest of the predictors seem to be statistically insignificant. The RSE is relatively high with 7.81, and the multiple R-squared 0.557 indicates that these four input variables explain nearly 60% of the variance in five_star ratings. Besides, F-statistic: 4.714 and 0.01154 provides modest evidence against H_0, hence there is at least one input variable explaining the output.

Computing the test statistic:

t <- sum((mat- expected)^2 / expected)
t
## [1] 666.8483

We obtained a test statistics of 666.8483, which is a large value. This is a sure inidcator for an extremely small p-value.

Despite the large t, we now go ahead calculate our p-value with chi-squared distribution, which is virtually zero:

degrees <- (nrow(mat)-1) * (ncol(mat)-1)
degrees
## [1] 116
1-pchisq(t, degrees)
## [1] 0
chisq.test(mat, simulate.p.value = TRUE)
## 
##  Pearson's Chi-squared test with simulated p-value (based on 2000
##  replicates)
## 
## data:  mat
## X-squared = 666.85, df = NA, p-value = 0.0004998

Our p-value is 0.0004998, which is a very strong evidence against \(H_{0}\).

Hence we have very convincing evidence against H_0: the probability of getting a certain rating differs between restaurants. An extremely small p-value is a surefire indicator that such differences in reviews dont occur by random chance only.
This clearly means some restaurants are more likely to be rated higher than other ones. As a conclusion we can say that the difference in rankings is meaningful, and customer ratings are an indicator for that.
